# Abstract 

Optical character recognition (OCR) and multilingual text understanding remain major failure modes of multimodal large language models (MLLMs), particularly in real-world images containing cluttered layouts, small fonts, blur, occlusion, and complex typography. We present an OCR-aware multilingual multimodal training framework that com-bines (i) large-scale synthetic OCR-to-translation data generation, (ii) OCR-aware supervised fine-tuning (SFT) with LoRA adaptation, and (iii) structured visual chain-of-thought (CoT) prompting for reasoning under uncertain visual condi-tions. Using a LLaMA-based multimodal architecture, the proposed framework substantially improves OCR completeness, multilingual translation accuracy, and robustness under degraded visual conditions. Experimental results on multilingual receipts, menus, posters, signs, handwritten text, and document images demonstrate significantly improved visual-text ground-ing compared with the baseline model. In particular, the proposed OCR-aware post-training framework improves extraction of small, blurred, spatially scattered, and partially occluded text while reducing reliance on language priors under un-certain OCR conditions. Qualitative comparisons with frontier multimodal sys-tems, including GPT-5-class and Gemini-family models, further suggest improved OCR grounding and reduced hallucination under noisy and visually ambiguous OCR scenarios. Overall, the results indicate that data-centric OCR-aware multimodal post-training provides an effective and scalable direction for improving multilingual OCR and OCR-based visual question answering systems. 

# 1 Introduction 

1.1 Background 

Text in images is ubiquitous in real-world environments, including menus, receipts, medication labels, street signs, posters, and official documents. Correctly reading and translating such text is es-sential for multimodal assistants and assistive AI systems. However, OCR in natural images remains fundamentally more challenging than traditional document OCR due to blur, glare, perspective dis-tortion, partial occlusion, cluttered layouts, and multilingual scripts. Despite substantial progress in vision–language models (VLMs), including LLaVA, Qwen-VL, PaLI-X, Kosmos-2, Gemini-family models, LLaMA-family multimodal systems, and GPT-4 and 5-class multimodal models, these systems still frequently hallucinate text, omit crucial tokens, or generate mistranslations under degraded visual conditions. In practice, we observe several recurring failure modes: (i) Small or degraded text may be skipped or inaccurately recognized. (ii) OCR content distributed across multiple image regions may be only partially extracted. (iii) Partially visible text may be autoregressively “completed” using language priors rather than grounded visual evidence. (iv) Numeric values may be hallucinated. Recent frontier multimodal systems partially mitigate some of these issues through explicit visual reasoning and tool-augmented inference strategies that analyze visible text before generating re-sponses. However, such reasoning pipelines are often computationally expensive in large-scale set-tings . 

1.2 Objective 

This paper investigates whether OCR robustness and multilingual text understanding in multimodal large language models (MLLMs) can be substantially improved through OCR-aware data curation and supervised post-training. We introduce two complementary approaches: (i) OCR-aware post-training using multilingual OCR datasets with LoRA-based supervised fine-tuning (SFT). (ii) A lightweight visual chain-of-thought (CoT) prompting strategy that provides auxiliary structured reasoning over visible text regions. 

# 2 Related Work 

2.1 OCR-Capable Multimodal Large Language Models 

Recent multimodal large language models (MLLMs), including Qwen-VL (Bai 2023),PaLI-X (chen 2023), LLaVA (Liu 2023), Kosmos-2 (Peng 2023), and mPLUG-Owl (Ye 2023), integrate visual encoders with large language models to enable multimodal understanding and OCR-related rea-soning. Although these systems demonstrate strong performance on multimodal benchmarks, they often remain sensitive to visual degradation such as blur, rotation, occlusion, cluttered layouts, and multilingual text, leading to incomplete OCR extraction and hallucinated text generation. 

2.2 Document and Scene-Text Benchmarks 

Benchmarks such as TextVQA (Singh 2019) and DocVQA (Mathew 2021) evaluate multimodal reasoning over scene text and document images. While these datasets significantly advanced OCR-oriented multimodal evaluation, they under-represent challenging in-the-wild conditions including severe blur, low-light imaging, perspective distortion, multilingual text mixtures, and scattered OCR regions commonly observed in mobile-camera environments. 

2.3 Visual Reasoning and Chain-of-Thought Prompting 

Recent multimodal systems have explored reasoning-guided inference strategies for improving OCR and visual understanding. Chain-of-thought (CoT) prompting (Wei 2022) chain-of-thought and mul-timodal reasoning frameworks such as MM-ReAct (Yang 2023) and Visual ChatGPT (Wu 2023) incorporate structured reasoning or external visual tools to improve perception under ambiguous visual conditions. However, these approaches are often computationally expensive and difficult to deploy efficiently at scale. 22.4 Data-Centric Multimodal Alignment 

Recent work has increasingly emphasized the importance of data-centric post-training, synthetic instruction generation, and preference optimization for improving multimodal grounding and re-ducing hallucination (Wang 2023, Rafailov 2023). Our work follows this direction by focusing on OCR-aware multilingual data curation and supervised multimodal post-training to improve OCR completeness, translation robustness, and hallucination reduction under degraded visual conditions. 

# 3 Methodologies 

3.1 Model Architecture 

We adopt an existing multimodal vision-language model (VLM) architecture inspired by recent large-scale systems such as AnyMal (Moon et al., 2024), Flamingo (Alayrac et al., 2022), and BLIP-2 (Li et al., 2023), with LLaMA-3 70B serving as the language backbone. Our work does not introduce a new multimodal architecture, additional OCR-specific recognition modules, or external OCR engines. Given an input image, a frozen MetaCLIP-based ViT encoder (Xu et al., 2023) extracts dense visual features, which are compressed into a fixed number of visual tokens through a Perceiver-based resampling module (Jaegle et al., 2021). The visual tokens are aligned with the language embedding space and concatenated with text tokens before being processed by the LLaMA decoder for end-to-end multimodal reasoning and generation, as illustrated in Figure 1. 

Figure 1: Model architecture: image features are embedded and aligned with text tokens as inputs to the LLaMA decoder for multimodal generation. During training, the LLaMA and Perceiver module weights are updated through backpropagation, while the ViT encoder remains frozen. Our framework does not rely on an external OCR model. 

Comparison with traditional OCR-based pipelines. Conventional vision-language systems of-ten rely on a modular OCR-centric pipeline, where text understanding is delegated to a dedicated OCR subsystem. This typically involves a multi-stage process: (i) text detection (e.g., EAST (Zhou et al., 2017), CRAFT (Baek et al., 2019)), (ii) text recognition (e.g., Tesseract (Smith, 2007), TrOCR (Li et al., 2021)), and (iii) structured encoding of recognized text (often including bounding boxes or layout metadata) before feeding into an LLM or downstream reasoning model. In some systems, external OCR services such as Google Vision OCR are also used as black-box components. 3While effective, such pipelines introduce several limitations, including increased latency, error prop-agation across stages, and sensitivity to noisy or redundant detections (e.g., over-fragmented bound-ing boxes or spurious text regions). Moreover, decoupling perception from reasoning can hinder joint optimization across modalities. Unlike some existing LLaMA3-based OCR systems that incorporate an additional OCR model for explicit text extraction before LLM reasoning, our framework removes the separate OCR module and learns OCR capability directly within the end-to-end multimodal model through OCR-aware post-training. This end-to-end design reduces system complexity, improves robustness to noisy visual inputs, and enables tighter coupling between visual perception and language reasoning. 

3.2 Problem Formulation 

We study multimodal reasoning in vision-language models under noisy OCR conditions, where textual information is implicitly embedded within visual inputs and must be inferred from degraded observations. Let I denote an image, Z latent OCR regions (e.g., text bounding boxes), T the underlying text extracted from those regions, and Y the target output (e.g., answer or translation). The overall task can be modeled as marginalizing over latent textual structure:

As a result, errors in latent text extraction propagate to downstream reasoning, and the model must operate under a highly uncertain latent-text bottleneck. In practice, modern multimodal language models approximate this distribution implicitly via autore-gressive decoding:

However, this factorization introduces a known limitation: when visual evidence is weak, the model may rely disproportionately on learned language priors rather than grounded OCR signals, leading to hallucinated or partially inferred text. Under visually degraded conditions, uncertainty in p (Z|I) increases, making reliable text grounding difficult and effectively weakening the connection between visual input and linguistic output. This mismatch between latent visual uncertainty and strong autoregressive priors is a primary driver of OCR omission and hallucination errors. To address this issue, the model is first optimized using supervised fine-tuning (SFT): 

LSFT = − log pθ (Y | I, q ), (3) which learns direct mappings from visual inputs to target outputs at scale. In most OCR scenarios, this objective provides strong multimodal alignment and constitutes the primary source of perfor-mance improvement in our framework. 3.3 Visual CoT Prompting 

To improve robustness under these challenging conditions, we additionally incorporate visual prompt chain-of-thought (CoT) reasoning as an auxiliary grounding mechanism. This motivates the introduction of reasoning-based supervision, where the model is encouraged to explicitly con-struct intermediate visual evidence before producing the final answer. We implement this using visual prompt CoT, introducing a latent reasoning trace R:

p(Y | I, q ) = X

> R

pθ (Y | R, I, q ) pθ (R | I, q ). (4) 4Rather than directly generating the final output from uncertain visual observations, the model first predicts an intermediate reasoning process: 

R ∼ pθ (R | I, q ), Y ∼ pθ (Y | R, I, q ). (5) This decomposition breaks the original multimodal reasoning problem into a sequence of simpler conditional inference steps. Under noisy OCR conditions, directly modeling p(Y | I, q ) often encourages the autoregressive decoder to rely on dominant language priors when visual evidence is incomplete. By introducing the latent reasoning state R, the model is encouraged to progressively ground visual evidence before final answer generation, reducing error propagation from uncertain OCR signals and mitigating hallucination. While the problem formulation defines an abstract reasoning variable R, in practice we instantiate 

R as an explicit language-based reasoning trace generated through structured prompting. Instead of directly predicting Y from (I, q ), the model is guided to produce intermediate reasoning before answering. Formally, the inference process can be viewed as: 

R = CoT (I, q ), Y = pθ (Y | I, q, R ), (6) where R is an explicitly generated visual-text reasoning trace induced by the prompt. This decom-position reduces direct dependence on weak OCR signals by forcing the model to first synthesize intermediate structured representations of visual text, improving robustness under uncertainty and reducing hallucination. Unlike standard system prompts used in supervised fine-tuning (e.g., “You are a helpful visual assis-tant. . . ”), we introduce structured step-by-step instructions that encourage OCR-aware visual rea-soning: • Examine the entire image to first understand the overall scene and global context. • If the question involves small, distant, or off-center objects or text, systematically search different image regions, including the foreground, background, left, and right areas, while focusing on potentially relevant details. • If the text appears blurry, low-contrast, partially occluded, or rotated, reason as if mentally focusing on, enhancing, or re-orienting the relevant region to improve readability. • When appropriate, briefly explain the visual evidence or reasoning process used to derive the answer. • Finally, provide a clear and precise answer grounded in the observed image evidence. This structured prompting induces R as an explicit intermediate reasoning trace, effectively decom-posing the inference process into (I, q ) → R → Y . This encourages stronger visual grounding on OCR content and reduces hallucination caused by reliance on language priors under ambiguous visual conditions. 

3.4 OCR Data Curation 

In addition to existing large-scale image-English OCR datasets, we further curate multilingual and visually challenging OCR scenarios that are relatively underrepresented in conventional OCR cor-pora. These include mixed-language composition, complex document layouts, rotated or low-contrast text, dense OCR regions, and degraded visual conditions commonly encountered in the wild scenes. To improve coverage of these long-tail OCR conditions, we combine real-world OCR data with scalable multilingual synthetic generation ( ∼5M samples). 

3.4.1 Multilingual OCR-to-Translation Data Generation 

We construct a synthetic OCR-grounded dataset designed to better approximate the joint distribution of visual appearance and multilingual textual content. The generation process is factorized as: 

Tsrc ∼ p(T ), I ∼ p(I | Tsrc ), Ttgt ∼ p(Ttgt | Tsrc ), (7) where Tsrc is the source OCR text T in English, and Ttgt is the OCR in target language. It yields the joint distribution: 

p(I, T src , T tgt ) = p(Tsrc ) p(I | Tsrc ) p(Ttgt | Tsrc ). (8) 5This formulation explicitly couples visual inputs with underlying OCR text and multilingual super-vision, ensuring that translation depends on shared latent linguistic content rather than purely visual correlation. To bridge the gap between synthetic and real-world OCR conditions, we apply realistic visual degra-dations such as blur, rotation, occlusion, perspective distortion, compression artifacts, and cluttered layouts. These transformations simulate failures in p(Z | I), allowing the model to learn robustness under noisy observation conditions. Our synthetic pipeline differs from standard text-to-image generation in two key aspects: • Text-first conditioning: Images are generated conditioned explicitly on sampled multilin-gual OCR text, enabling controlled variation in typography, layout structure, and language mixing. • Context-aware rendering: Backgrounds are generated to remain semantically consistent with embedded text, ensuring realistic co-occurrence between scene context and OCR con-tent. This design induces a training distribution that more closely matches in-the-wild OCR uncertainty, improving visual-text alignment and reducing reliance on language priors when visual evidence is incomplete. Consequently, the model learns more faithful grounding and exhibits reduced halluci-nation in low-quality visual settings. 

3.4.2 Modular Generative Visual Translation Framework 

In addition to multilingual OCR-aware data generation, we introduce a Modular Generative Visual Translation framework for in-situ OCR text replacement (Figure2–3). Unlike end-to-end generative approaches, which jointly entangle scene synthesis and text generation, our framework decomposes the translation process into three controllable stages: (i) Segmentation (explicit localization): Pixel-level text masks are generated using DBNet or SAM 2, enabling precise localization and spatial grounding of OCR regions. (ii) Inpainting (background preservation): Generative inpainting reconstructs the underlying background surface after text removal, isolating textual content from surrounding scene structures. (iii) Rendering (deterministic text insertion): Translated text is rendered with explicit geomet-ric and stylistic control using homography transformation and alpha blending, preserving perspective consistency and typographic alignment. This modular decomposition introduces structural inductive bias largely absent in prior end-to-end diffusion-based approaches. By explicitly separating localization, background reconstruction, and text rendering, the framework reduces probabilistic text corruption while preserving non-textual scene elements and maintaining typographic consistency under perspective distortion, blur, and vi-sual noise. Compared with fully generative OCR translation pipelines, the proposed framework provides stronger controllability and visual fidelity, particularly in scenarios requiring accurate multilingual text replacement and spatially grounded OCR reconstruction. 

3.5 Comparative Methodology Analysis 

Prior E2E diffusion approaches (Figure 2) are optimized for semantic generation, where global coherence is prioritized over local fidelity. While effective for creative synthesis, they are funda-mentally ill-suited for OCR translation tasks, as they treat text as part of the generative distribution and thus remain susceptible to hallucination and typographic errors. In contrast, our modular framework (Figure 3) explicitly separates what to preserve (background) from what to modify (text). This separation enables deterministic control over text generation while maintaining visual consistency, addressing a key limitation of prior MLLM-based OCR systems. # 4 Experimental Setup 

4.1 Training Setup 

The proposed OCR-aware multimodal model is trained using LoRA-based supervised fine-tuning (SFT) on the OCR-image question-answer samples consisting of multilingual receipts, menus, 7posters, signs, handwritten text, and document images. The training corpus combines both the public and author-curated synthetic-OCR data generated through the proposed multilingual OCR-to-translation pipeline at large scale. Training is performed using the AdamW optimizer with dynamic learning rate scheduling, includ-ing linear warm-up followed by cosine decay. LoRA adapters are applied with a rank of 512 for parameter-efficient multimodal adaptation. To improve robustness under practical OCR conditions, training images are augmented with mul-tiple visual degradations including blur, low resolution, rotation, perspective distortion, occlusion, compression artifacts, cluttered layouts, and multilingual text mixtures. 

4.2 Evaluation Metrics 

We evaluate model performance using three primary metrics: (i) OCR Completeness: percentage of correctly recovered OCR tokens, particularly for long-context and spatially scattered OCR content. (ii) Hallucination Rate: frequency of visually unsupported or incorrectly generated OCR con-tent. (iii) Translation Quality: multilingual OCR translation accuracy measured using one-gram BLEU score. Evaluation is conducted on challenging in-the-wild OCR scenarios including multilingual receipts, restaurant menus, posters, street signs, handwritten notes, and document images under degraded visual conditions. 

4.3 Baselines and Comparative Evaluation 

We compare the proposed OCR-aware SFT model, LlAMA3 VLM OCR-SFT against the origi-nal baseline multimodal model without OCR-aware post-training - LlAMA3 VLM. In addition, qualitative comparisons are conducted against frontier multimodal systems including GPT-4-class multimodal models and Gemini-family models on challenging OCR and multilingual reasoning sce-narios.

These examples are not intended as a standalone benchmark; rather, they highlight failure modes that are less visible in clean cases, where most models perform similarly well. The selected cases emphasize degraded OCR, multilingual text, complex layouts, ambiguous visual evidence, and grounding-sensitive rea-soning.

Hallucination versus completeness trade-off: Gemini generally produces the most detailed re-sponses, frequently extracting fine-grained information such as layout structure, contextual meta-data, and scene-level descriptions. However, this increased completeness is often accompanied by higher hallucination rates, where visually unsupported details are introduced (e.g., incorrect license plate numbers or fabricated contextual information in tourism brochures). In contrast, GPT exhibits more conservative behavior but still produces hallucinations under ambiguous OCR conditions, par-ticularly when text is small, noisy, or partially occluded. 10 Robustness of OCR grounding: The LLaMA-based model fine-tuned using our OCR-aware data curation pipeline demonstrates stronger visual grounding and improved robustness under degraded OCR conditions. Compared with Gemini, the model introduces fewer visually unsupported details, while compared with GPT, it shows stronger robustness under noisy, low-contrast, or handwritten OCR scenarios (e.g., see Figure 6). 

Behavior under ambiguity: In challenging settings such as handwritten menus, mirrored text, blur, or partial occlusion, all models exhibit performance degradation. However, distinct failure patterns emerge: Gemini tends to over-interpret uncertain visual evidence, GPT often substitutes plausible but incorrect text using language priors, whereas the fine-tuned LLaMA model generally produces simpler but more visually faithful outputs. 

Safety and refusal behavior: GPT uniquely demonstrates stronger refusal behavior in safety-sensitive scenarios, whereas other models may still attempt to provide answers, sometimes incor-rectly. This behavior likely reflects differences in alignment and safety policies rather than OCR capability alone. 

Overall comparison: Aggregating observations across all examples, Gemini achieves the highest response completeness but also exhibits the highest hallucination tendency. GPT is generally more conservative but less robust under noisy OCR conditions. The fine-tuned LLaMA model provides a stronger balance between OCR completeness and visual faithfulness, substantially reducing halluci-nation while maintaining competitive extraction quality across diverse in-the-wild scenarios. # 6 Conclusion 

This work investigates the limitations of current multimodal large language models (MLLMs) un-der practical OCR conditions and proposes an OCR-aware multimodal post-training framework to improve multilingual OCR robustness and reduce hallucination. By combining multilingual OCR-aware data curation, large-scale synthetic OCR generation, LoRA-based supervised fine-tuning (SFT), and structured visual reasoning prompts, the proposed approach substantially improves OCR completeness, multilingual translation accuracy, and robustness under degraded visual conditions. Experimental results demonstrate both quantitative and qualitative improvements across multilin-gual receipts, menus, posters, signs, handwritten text, and document images. Compared with the baseline model, the proposed framework recovers more detailed OCR content while significantly reducing hallucination under blur, occlusion, rotation, low-contrast, and cluttered visual environ-ments. Our analysis further suggests that hallucination in OCR-oriented MLLMs is closely related to weak visual grounding under uncertain or incomplete visual evidence. OCR-aware post-training improves robustness by encouraging stronger alignment between visual observations and generated text, re-ducing reliance on language priors when OCR uncertainty is high. Overall, the results indicate that data-centric OCR-aware multimodal training provides an effective and scalable direction for improving practical multilingual OCR systems and OCR-based visual question answering. Future work may explore stronger teacher-student distillation pipelines, larger multilingual OCR datasets, preference-based multimodal alignment, and integration with region-aware visual encoders to further improve OCR fidelity, robustness, and scalability.
